The PT Line — An Australian Commuting Story

A guided analysis of Australia’s public transport landscape for the average Australian. We use 2021 Census data to see who leads the nation in commuting, then explore operational data from Victoria and South Australia to understand the real-world patterns behind the numbers.

Station 1 — State of Play: Who Leads and Who Lags?

The 2021 Census data reveals a clear divide: states with major, rail-anchored cities like Sydney and Melbourne show significantly higher public transport use. The lollipop chart ranks each state, clearly identifying the leaders, while the diverging bar chart powerfully illustrates how far each state sits from the national average.

Station 2 — The National Picture: What We Ride and Where

Nationally, trains form the backbone of commuting, carrying the largest share of passengers. Buses play a vital role in extending network reach, while trams serve dense urban corridors. This geographic pattern is clear on the map: usage is heavily concentrated in the south-eastern states, where extensive heavy rail infrastructure underpins the system.

Next stop: Access vs Action →

Station 3 — Access vs. Action: Does More Infrastructure Mean More Riders?

There appears to be a positive correlation: states with more stops and stations per capita tend to have higher PT usage. The scatter plot shows this upward trend. However, outliers like Tasmania—with high stop density but low ridership—reveal that infrastructure alone isn't enough. Service quality, frequency, and network integration are critical for converting access into action.

Next stop: Operational Pulse →

Station 4 — Operational Pulse: A Tale of Two Cities

Operational data brings the story to life. In Victoria, the line chart of monthly patronage vividly shows the dramatic shock of the COVID-19 pandemic and the slow, uneven recovery across different modes. In Adelaide, the data reveals a system heavily reliant on buses and a strong weekly commuting rhythm, with smartcard taps peaking from Monday to Friday.

Final stop: Takeaways →

Station 5 — Key Takeaways

  • Rail-centred metros set the pace. States with large, established heavy rail networks (NSW, VIC) have a significant advantage in public transport commuting.
  • Trains dominate, buses extend reach. Nationally, trains perform the heavy lifting for high-capacity corridors, while buses are essential for broader network coverage.
  • Access is a prerequisite, but service is key. While having more stops correlates with higher usage, service quality—frequency, reliability, and integration—is what converts potential riders into regulars.
  • Operational data validates the story. Real-world tap and patronage data confirms the high-level patterns seen in the Census, from weekday commuting rhythms to the long-term impacts of major events.

Data Sources & Methodology

This visualisation combines several publicly available datasets to build a comprehensive picture of public transport in Australia. All data processing was performed to create clean, lightweight files suitable for web visualisation.

Core National Data

  • Commuting Patterns: Sourced from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) 2021 Census of Population and Housing, specifically the “Method of Travel to Work (MTWP)” dataset. This provides state-level percentages and national mode splits.
  • Population Data: Estimated Resident Population (ERP) data from the ABS for December 2021 was used to calculate per-capita metrics.
  • Geographic Data: State and territory boundaries were sourced from Natural Earth (Admin 1 level) and converted to TopoJSON format for use in the map.

Infrastructure & Operational Data

  • Infrastructure Density: Calculated from General Transit Feed Specification (GTFS) data published by state transit authorities (DataVic, Translink QLD, data.sa.gov.au, Transperth). Stops and parent stations were deduplicated to create a proxy for infrastructure availability per 100,000 residents.
  • Victorian Patronage: Sourced from the Victorian Department of Transport and Planning’s “Monthly public transport patronage by mode” CSV file, covering metropolitan train, tram, and bus services.
  • Adelaide Validations: Sourced from data.sa.gov.au, using the “Banded Adelaide Metrocard Validations” datasets for all four quarters of 2021. These files were combined and processed to create the full 52-week heatmap data.
